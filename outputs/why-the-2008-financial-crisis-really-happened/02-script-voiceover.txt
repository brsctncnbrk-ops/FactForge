September 15th, 2008. A 158-year-old investment bank opens for business one last time — and doesn't make it to lunch.

Lehman Brothers held six hundred thirty-nine billion dollars in assets. It's still the largest bankruptcy in American history.

To understand how one bank's collapse nearly broke the world, you have to go back further — to how the bubble formed.

First, cheap mortgages inflated a housing bubble. Then home prices stopped rising, and borrowers began to default.

The mortgage bonds built on those loans turned toxic — and the losses spread through the entire banking system.

By September, banks no longer trusted each other. Lending between them nearly stopped overnight.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost more than half its value in just seventeen months.

To stop the bleeding, Congress authorized seven hundred billion dollars to rescue the banking system.

Lehman was allowed to fail. Days later, the government spent one hundred eighty-two billion dollars to save a single insurance company: AIG.

Here's what almost nobody remembers: TARP's final cost wasn't seven hundred billion. It was about thirty-one billion.

And the AIG bailout? Fully repaid — with a twenty-two point seven billion dollar profit for taxpayers.

But the rescue didn't reach everyone. Unemployment doubled to ten percent within two years.

Regulators shut down four hundred fourteen banks across the country in just four years.

One bank's Monday morning collapse. A decade of consequences. That's how 2008 really happened.
